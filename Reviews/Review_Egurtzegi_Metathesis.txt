\section*{1. Scope and positioning of the chapter}

Ander Egurtzegi’s forthcoming chapter offers one of the most comprehensive syntheses of metathesis to date. Rather than treating metathesis as a single phenomenon, he frames it as a heterogeneous family of processes unified only by their surface outcome. This perspective aligns with the foundational work of Blevins \& Garrett (1998, 2004), yet Egurtzegi’s treatment is broader, more empirically grounded, and more explicit in its theoretical commitments.

From the outset, Egurtzegi states: \textit{``Metathesis is far from a monolithic sound change, but a cover term for many different types of synchronic and diachronic changes.''}

\section*{2. What the chapter inherits from Blevins \& Garrett}

Egurtzegi builds on several key insights from Blevins \& Garrett: the typological distinction between perceptual, compensatory, coarticulatory, and auditory metathesis; the recognition that some metatheses are phonetically motivated; and the idea that many apparent metatheses are telescoped sequences involving feature spreading and deletion. These foundations remain central, but Egurtzegi identifies limitations and extends the framework.

\section*{3. Egurtzegi’s new contributions}

\subsection*{3.1. Regularity and gradualness}

A major contribution is the demonstration---based on acoustic and articulatory evidence---that certain metatheses behave like fully regular Neogrammarian sound changes. The Andalusian shift from pre-aspiration to post-aspiration is treated with unprecedented detail, showing synchronic stages, generational progression, extension across word boundaries, and stable perception.

\subsection*{3.2. Reciprocal metathesis}

Egurtzegi introduces a new category, \textit{reciprocal metathesis}, linked to motor-plan exchange mechanisms. This model accounts for full segmental swaps, certain non-local exchanges, and phenomena resembling speech-error dynamics---a domain where Blevins \& Garrett lacked a satisfactory model.

\subsection*{3.3. Local vs.\ non-local metathesis}

Egurtzegi clarifies that local metathesis can be highly regular, while non-local metathesis is rarely regular but may mimic regularity through strong perceptual biases. This resolves several ambiguities inherited from earlier typologies.

\subsection*{3.4. Instrumental evidence}

The chapter integrates spectrograms, waveform analyses, generational comparisons, perception studies, and fine-grained coarticulatory data, allowing metathesis to be observed in real time rather than inferred retrospectively.

\section*{4. Theoretical contribution}

Egurtzegi proposes a modular view: metathesis is not a type of change but a possible outcome of several distinct phonetic, perceptual, and cognitive mechanisms. This resolves long-standing contradictions such as regular vs.\ irregular, local vs.\ distant, and natural vs.\ psychological.

\section*{5. Conclusion}

This chapter is a major update to the theory of metathesis. It extends Blevins \& Garrett but also stabilizes the typology, demonstrates the possibility of regularity, broadens the explanatory range, and documents modern cases with unprecedented instrumental precision. For diachronic research---and especially for Pre-Basque analyses---this chapter becomes an essential reference.
